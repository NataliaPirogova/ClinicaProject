About clinical trials


Why are clinical trials conducted?

Clinical trials are part of a long process of developing a new drug that can take many years.

First, developers and scientists study a new drug in the laboratory. Then, if possible, use it in experimental models of diseases in animals. If a new treatment shows promise, researchers move on to the next step, conducting clinical trials in humans.

Every year, tens of thousands of studies are conducted worldwide, involving millions of patients.

All members of the research teams have the necessary training and are trained in GCP (Good Clinical Practice).

The main benefit of participating in a clinical trial is that you will be able to access new drugs that may work better than existing ones.

No one knows how safe and effective a new drug is - it is in order to find out that a study is being conducted. However, it must be understood that studies of a particular drug move to the clinical stage only after preclinical trials have been successfully completed: laboratory experiments in test tubes, on human cell cultures, on mice, on other animals. Conducting human trials is expensive, and pharmaceutical companies only venture into clinical trials if they believe in the possibility of success and have studied the safety of the drug.

The principles of voluntariness and the fullest possible informing of participants are the basis of modern standards of clinical research. The ethical principles on which research should be conducted are set out in the Declaration of Helsinki of the World Medical Association, adopted in 1964 and regularly updated since then. The consent of the participants, according to the document, should not only be completely voluntary, but can also be withdrawn at any time without explanation. In addition, everyone who agrees to become part of the study must clearly understand: what exactly is being researched, what is his own interest to participate in and what risks does he bear in doing so.

Participation in a study is only necessary when the expected benefits of using a new drug may outweigh the expected risks. Whether this is true or not in your case, you will have to decide for yourself, after consulting with your doctor.

Sometimes, especially in severe cases, participation in research is the only chance. If existing, already registered drugs do not work in your case, then only palliative care remains, or participation in a clinical trial. Then even a small chance of success is better than nothing.

In addition to this main argument, there are additional advantages: participants in clinical trials undergo thorough diagnostics, treatment protocols are scrupulously observed during their treatment (usually developed by the world's best specialists), throughout the entire period of participation in the study, you are under close supervision of specialists participating in the study.

The financial issue should not be forgotten either: you will not be charged for treatment with new drugs. All examinations will also be carried out free of charge within a strict time frame.
